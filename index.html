Our Journey

126 Years, Five Defining Moments

From a single Geneva workshop to a global maison — without ever losing sight of the bench where it began.

1898

The Founding

Émile Stackly opens his Geneva atelier on Rue du Rhône, determined to build Europe's most accurate pocket chronometer.

1932

First In-House Tourbillon

Our inaugural tourbillon stuns the Basel Salon — watchmakers travel from across Europe to examine the mechanism.

1969

Chronometer Record

The Calibre XVIII sets a COSC accuracy benchmark that stands unbeaten for over a decade.

2004

Bespoke Commissions Launch

No catalogue, no compromise — every commission built to a single patron's brief over 18 to 36 months.

2026

The Modern Maison

32 boutiques, 18 countries, one uncompromising standard — and 900,000 collectors who refuse to settle.

Raw Materials

Only the Earth's Finest

From grade-5 titanium to platinum 950 — each case alloy chosen not just for beauty, but for the weight it carries on a wrist.

18k Rose Gold

Warm Lustre

An alloy of gold, copper, and silver — deeper in hue than yellow gold, warmer than white. Preferred by collectors for its patina over decades.

Platinum 950

The Rarest Metal

Thirty times rarer than gold. Naturally white, hypoallergenic, and so dense it seems to resist time itself. Reserved for our most complex complications.

Grade-5 Titanium

Aerospace Strength

Used in space shuttle components. At just 4.5g/cm³, it halves the weight of steel while tripling its tensile strength. The choice for active collectors.

316L Steel

The Everyman's Noble

Medical-grade stainless steel, hand-polished and satin-brushed in alternating facets. The entry point into STACKLY — and the hardest to walk past.

The Process

From Raw Metal to a Masterpiece

Every STACKLY timepiece passes through four centuries-old stages before it earns the right to bear our name.

01

Movement Design

Each calibre begins as a blueprint — hundreds of components engineered to tolerances measured in microns.

02

Component Finishing

Bridges and plates are anglaged, perlaged, and Côtes de Genève decorated by hand — each surface taking days.

03

Hand Assembly

A single master watchmaker assembles the entire movement — one set of hands for one masterpiece.

04

Certification

Sixteen days of continuous testing across five positions and three temperatures before any watch is released.
